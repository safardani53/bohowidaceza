LEGENDARY Canadian singer-songwriter Leonard Cohen has died aged 82 - news that has rocked millions of music fans.

Regarded as a modern day poet and master lyricist, his songs moved people in a way that very few musicians have been able to.

He was even touted as a visionary by many people and several famous musicians have been influenced by his easy yet profound style of lyric-writing.

Cohen's rich lyrics are dark, although he always claimed he was no more depressed than anyone else.

A statement appeared on Cohen’s Facebook page on Thursday night announcing the loss of “one of music’s most revered and prolific visionaries”.

It added that a memorial will take place in Los Angeles at a later date.

What song is he best known for?

Almost definitely Hallelujah.

The song has been covered by dozens of stars including Bono, Alexandra Burke and KD Lang.

However, virtually nobody paid attention to the track when it kicked off the second side of his LP Various Positions in 1984.

Cohen, however, knew he had something special.

He spent an unusually long time on the lyrics, obsessing over every word and going through 80 different drafts.

When the Velvet Underground's John Cale asked him to send over the lyrics so he could cover it, he received a 15-page fax full of discarded verses.

Cale cobbled together a new version of the song, which he recorded on the piano.

It was that version that Jeff Buckley covered on his 1994 LP Grace, and slowly the song became an absolute sensation.

 

What's 'So Long, Marianne' all about?

Long before he was famous, Cohen met and fell for a beautiful Norwegian woman called Marianne Ihlen who would his lover and muse for many years.

She was married to writer Axel Jensen, but the attraction between Ihlen and Cohen was intense and they eventually they found room in their lives for each other.

She inspired many of his most passionate love songs off his early albums, including  'So Long, Marianne.'

What about 'Bird on a Wire'?

Leonard Cohen was going through a bout of depression while living on the Greek Island of Hydra with Marianne when he noticed a bird sitting by himself on a telephone wire.

He began writing a poem comparing himself to the lonely bird, but it would be a long time before he turned it into a song.

During the 1968 Songs From a Room sessions in Nashville, Cohen ran through the song over and over until he got so frustrated he gave up.

Days before the final session, he simply walked up to the microphone and nailed it.

His hit Anthem also features birds and the lyric 'There is a crack in everything That's how the light gets in'.

The line is said to refer to the fact that not everything if perfect in life. It is believed to another reference to his love for Marianne.

Surely 'Famous Blue Raincoat' can't be about a coat?

Kind of.

Sometime in the early 1970s, a thief stole Cohen's old raincoat from Marianne's  apartment.

It was that very coat that inspired Cohen to write one of his most beloved and mysterious songs.

It's written in the form of a letter, possibly to the narrator's brother, who stole his lover.
